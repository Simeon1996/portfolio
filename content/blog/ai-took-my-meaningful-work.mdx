There is a specific kind of loss that nobody is naming cleanly, and I want to try.

It is not the loss of a job. It is not the loss of relevance, or income, or status — though those fears are real and worth their own conversation. This is something smaller and more personal. It is the loss of the work inside the work. The part that made the rest of it worth tolerating.

I spent years getting good at debugging. Not the kind where the stack trace points at the line. The other kind — the three-hour session where the error is somewhere upstream of where it appears, where you hold a mental model of five interacting systems in your head and reason your way to the one place something could go wrong. It felt like detective work. It felt, honestly, like the best version of thinking I was capable of.

AI is good at that now. Better than me, on a lot of it.

And I notice something in myself that I did not expect: grief.

*The overlap is shrinking. That's the thing to grieve.*

---

The productivity discourse focuses on the wrong circle. "AI is commoditizing boilerplate, CRUD, tests, documentation." True. But those were never the circles I was attached to. That work was fine. I did it because it came with the job.

What AI reached for first — and this is the part that stings — was not the tedious work. It was the *interesting* work. The code review that required taste. The architecture decision that required intuition built over years of things breaking in unexpected ways. The refactor where you saw the elegant path and took it and felt, for a moment, like you were good at something.

Those tasks attracted AI attention precisely because they carry the highest signal. High value, complex reasoning, experienced judgment — of course that's where the capability landed first. But that is also where the meaning lived.

Here is a small, undramatic version of what I mean:

```python
# This used to take me an afternoon. Claude did it in 8 seconds.

# Before: three functions with duplicated validation logic,
# inconsistent error handling, and a subtle off-by-one
# in the pagination that had survived four code reviews.

# After: clean, correct, readable — and I didn't write it.
```

Not triumphant. Just factual.

---

The honest version of the fear is not "I will lose my job." It is: "I will keep my job, and the part of it that made me feel like myself will quietly disappear, and what remains will be the part I was enduring."

That's specific enough to grieve. It is not catastrophizing. It is accurate description.

I want to sit with that for a moment without rushing past it to the solution. Because the solution people reach for — "learn the new tools, find the new meaningful work, adapt" — is probably right as career advice. I wrote [a whole post about that](/blog/the-programmer-who-refused-to-change). But it skips over the thing that needs to be said first, which is: it is okay to feel the loss. The work mattered. The fact that a system can now replicate parts of it does not retroactively make it small.

---

But here is the harder question, the one I keep turning over:

What was the meaning actually made of?

When I was three hours into a debugging session and I found it — was the meaning in the *task*? Or was it in what the task required of me? The sustained attention, the willingness to hold uncertainty, the pattern recognition that only works if you've been burned before, the moment where the model in your head finally matched reality?

The task was the container. The meaning was the demand.

And that distinction matters, because if the meaning was always in what the work demanded of you — the quality of attention it required, the growth it forced, the problems it refused to make easy — then the question is not "where do I find that same work?" The question is "what demands that of me now?"

Harder problems exist. AI systems need people who can reason about what they should and shouldn't do, not just whether they compile. Teaching remains stubbornly human. Building the tools themselves is still genuinely difficult. The debugging session that used to live in a single codebase now lives in the space between the model and the world, which is weirder and more interesting than anything I was debugging before.

I do not know if that is consolation or just displacement. Maybe both.

What I know is this: the work was never the meaning. The meaning was what the work demanded of you. The grief is real. So is what comes next.
